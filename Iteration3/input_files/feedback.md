The current analysis suffers from a fundamental mismatch between the chosen model (Energy Manifold) and the physical reality of the damped harmonic oscillator. You are attempting to estimate parameters ($m, b$) using an energy-based proxy that is inherently redundant and ill-conditioned.

**1. Methodological Flaw: The Energy Manifold Trap**
The energy equation $E(t) = 0.5mv^2 + 0.5kx^2$ is a derived quantity, not a primitive observation. By using it for MLE, you are propagating noise through non-linear transformations ($v^2, x^2$), which artificially inflates variance and creates the "efficiency gap" you observed. Furthermore, the energy manifold is insensitive to the phase $\phi$ and the sign of the velocity, discarding critical information present in the raw $x(t)$ and $v(t)$ signals. The "local minima" issues in your optimizer are a direct consequence of this information loss.

**2. Critique of the Information Horizon ($T_H$)**
Your binary thresholding for $T_H$ is a significant analytical failure. Defining $T_H$ as a fixed percentage of ground truth is arbitrary and ignores the exponential nature of the decay. You are essentially trying to measure a "time to convergence" on a signal that is vanishing into the noise floor. Instead of a binary $T_H$, you should characterize the **Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) decay rate** as a function of $\zeta$.

**3. Actionable Improvements for Future Iterations:**
*   **Abandon the Energy Manifold for Estimation:** Stop using $E(t)$ for parameter estimation. Perform MLE directly on the displacement equation $x(t) = A e^{-\gamma t} \cos(\omega t + \phi) + \epsilon$. This is a standard non-linear regression problem that is far more stable and avoids the $v^2$ noise amplification.
*   **Reparameterize for Identifiability:** The coupling between $m$ and $b$ is exacerbated by your current formulation. Use the physical parameters $\gamma = b/2m$ and $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ as your primary variables. These are the parameters that actually govern the dynamics; $m$ and $b$ can be derived post-hoc. This will decouple the sensitivity matrix and likely resolve your ill-conditioned FIM.
*   **Replace $T_H$ with a Continuous Metric:** Instead of a binary $T_H$, calculate the **Fisher Information Rate** $\mathcal{I}(t) = \frac{d}{dt} \text{Tr}(\mathbf{F}(t))$. This provides a continuous measure of how much information is being harvested at any time $t$. The point where $\mathcal{I}(t)$ falls below the noise floor $\sigma_E$ is your true information horizon.
*   **Address the "Efficiency Gap":** The gap between your MLE and the CRLB is likely due to the optimizer failing to account for the heteroscedasticity of the noise in the energy domain. If you must use energy, you must use a Weighted Least Squares (WLS) approach where weights are $1/\sigma_E^2(t)$. However, moving to the displacement domain (as suggested above) is the superior, simpler, and more robust path.

**Summary:** You are currently fighting the math of your own derived variables. By shifting the estimation to the raw displacement signal and using $\gamma$ and $\omega$ as the primary parameters, you will eliminate the convergence issues, remove the need for arbitrary thresholds, and achieve a statistically sound estimate of the system's physical properties.
