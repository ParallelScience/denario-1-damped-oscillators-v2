**1. Critical Weakness: Misinterpretation of Logarithmic Sensitivity**
The reported sensitivity values ($S \approx 10^7$) are physically implausible for a system where energy $E(t) \propto A^2 e^{-2\gamma t}$. The logarithmic sensitivity $\frac{\partial \ln E}{\partial \ln p}$ for $p=b$ should be of order unity. The reported values suggest a calculation error—likely a failure to normalize the sensitivity by the energy magnitude or an incorrect derivative implementation (e.g., calculating the absolute change in energy rather than the relative change). If $S$ is truly $10^7$, the model is effectively claiming that a 1% change in $b$ results in a 100,000% change in energy, which contradicts the governing equations. You must re-verify the implementation of the sensitivity gradient; it is currently providing misleading "noise" rather than physical insight.

**2. Redundant and Misleading Fisher Information Analysis**
The Fisher Information Rate (FIR) analysis is currently a "black box" metric. You define the limit of identifiability as the point where FIR falls below the noise floor, but you have not demonstrated that this threshold is physically meaningful. Since the noise $\sigma_x^2$ is constant, the FIR decay is essentially just a function of the signal amplitude decay. This is circular: you are essentially proving that "when the signal disappears, we can no longer estimate parameters." This does not provide new scientific insight. 

**3. Missed Opportunity: The "Energy Manifold" Geometry**
The current plan treats the 20 oscillators as independent points. Instead of a heatmap of residuals, you should analyze the *curvature* of the energy manifold. The sensitivity to $b$ is inherently coupled to the time $t$ and the damping ratio $\zeta$. Rather than just reporting that "higher damping is more sensitive," you should quantify the *rate of divergence* as a function of $\zeta$. Is there a critical damping ratio $\zeta_c$ where the sensitivity transitions from linear to non-linear? Identifying this transition would be a much stronger scientific contribution than the current sensitivity table.
