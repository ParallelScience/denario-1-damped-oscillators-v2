The current analysis provides a useful mapping of the predictive horizon $T_d$, but it suffers from a significant methodological conflation between "model divergence" and "signal decay." By defining $T_d$ as the point where the residual exceeds the noise floor, you are essentially measuring the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) decay rather than the intrinsic sensitivity of the physical model.

**Critical Weaknesses:**
1. **Conflation of Noise and Sensitivity:** In an underdamped system, the total energy $E(t)$ decays exponentially. As $t \to 20s$, $E(t)$ approaches the noise floor $\sigma_{noise}$. Your $T_d$ metric is heavily biased by the fact that the signal itself is vanishing. A model will "diverge" simply because the signal is lost in noise, not necessarily because the Jacobian sensitivity is high.
2. **Arbitrary Perturbation Range:** You fixed $\delta_m, \delta_b \in [-0.1, 0.1]$. This is a "black box" approach that ignores the physical constraints of the system. The sensitivity of the energy to $b$ is time-dependent ($t \cdot e^{-2\gamma t}$), whereas sensitivity to $m$ is scale-dependent. Treating them as a uniform 10% perturbation masks the physical reality that $b$ dominates the dissipation rate while $m$ dominates the oscillation frequency.
3. **Missing "High" Damping Regime:** You noted the absence of high-damping oscillators. If the dataset is fixed, you must analyze the existing 20 oscillators more granularly. The current grouping (Low vs. Medium) is too coarse to reveal the non-linear transition points in the sensitivity manifold.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Normalize by Signal Amplitude:** Instead of using the raw noise floor $\sigma_{noise}(t)$, compute the *relative* error $\epsilon(t) = |E_{obs} - E_{model}| / E_{model}(t)$. This will decouple the sensitivity analysis from the absolute energy decay, allowing you to identify if the model is truly failing or if the signal is simply too weak to validate the model.
2. **Refine the Sensitivity Metric:** Replace the binary $T_d$ search with a "Sensitivity Index" $S(t) = \|\nabla E(t)\|_2$. Plot $S(t)$ against time for different $\zeta$ values. This will show *when* the model is most vulnerable to parameter errors, independent of the noise floor.
3. **Leverage the Jacobian:** You derived the Jacobian but used it only for a Taylor expansion at $T=20s$. Use the Jacobian to perform a **Global Sensitivity Analysis (Sobol indices)**. This will quantify what percentage of the energy variance is attributable to $m$ vs. $b$ at different stages of the oscillation. This is far more insightful than a simple $T_d$ scatter plot.
4. **Shift Focus to "Information Density":** Instead of just reporting $T_d$, report the "Information Horizon"—the time at which the sensitivity to $b$ becomes indistinguishable from the sensitivity to $m$ given the noise. This provides a fundamental limit on parameter identifiability, which is a much stronger scientific claim than a simple predictive horizon.

By shifting from a "divergence" perspective to an "identifiability" perspective, you will move from a descriptive observation of noise to a rigorous physical insight into the limits of parameter estimation in dissipative systems.
