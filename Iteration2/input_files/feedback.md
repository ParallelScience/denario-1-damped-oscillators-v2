The current analysis provides a useful sensitivity mapping but suffers from a significant conceptual conflation between "sensitivity of the model to parameters" and "identifiability of parameters from data."

**Critique of Methodology:**
1. **Conflation of Sensitivity and Identifiability:** The study calculates the sensitivity of the *model* $E(t, m, b)$ to its parameters. However, the report interprets this as a limit on "parameter identifiability." Sensitivity is a necessary but not sufficient condition for identifiability. High sensitivity to $b$ at $t > T_H$ actually implies that the data is *more* informative about $b$ in that regime, not less. The "Information Horizon" is currently defined as a point of vulnerability, whereas it is mathematically the point where the data becomes most useful for constraining the damping coefficient.
2. **Redundancy of DGSM:** Using DGSM to approximate Sobol indices is mathematically elegant but overkill for a system where the analytical Jacobian is already computed. The Jacobian $\mathbf{J}(t)$ provides the local sensitivity directly. The transition to DGSM adds complexity without providing new insights into the underlying physics.
3. **Signal Processing Bias:** The use of a Savitzky-Golay filter on the energy signal is a potential source of systematic error. Energy is a non-linear transformation of $x$ and $v$. Filtering the energy signal directly can introduce artificial damping or phase shifts that are not present in the raw displacement/velocity data.

**Actionable Recommendations for Future Iterations:**
1. **Shift Focus to Fisher Information:** Instead of the sensitivity ratio $R(t)$, compute the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) for the parameters $(m, b)$. The inverse of the FIM provides the Cramer-Rao Lower Bound (CRLB) on parameter variance. This will provide a rigorous, statistically grounded "Information Horizon" based on the signal-to-noise ratio of the provided dataset, rather than a heuristic ratio of partial derivatives.
2. **Direct Parameter Estimation:** Validate the "Information Horizon" by performing a sliding-window parameter estimation (e.g., using a simple Levenberg-Marquardt or MLE fit) on the data. Compare the variance of the estimated $\hat{b}$ and $\hat{m}$ as a function of the window end-time. If the theory holds, the variance of $\hat{b}$ should drop significantly after $T_H$. This moves the project from theoretical sensitivity to empirical validation.
3. **Remove Signal Filtering:** Abandon the Savitzky-Golay filter on the energy signal. Since the dataset provides $x$ and $v$ directly, calculate $E(t)$ from the raw data. If noise is an issue, perform the sensitivity analysis on the residuals of the fit rather than filtering the energy signal itself.
4. **Re-evaluate the "Vulnerability" Claim:** Reframe the conclusion. High sensitivity to $b$ at late times is a feature, not a bug. It means the system's long-term behavior is a "sensor" for the damping coefficient. The "vulnerability" is only present if the measurement noise is high enough to obscure the signal at those late times. Quantify this by comparing the sensitivity $S_b(t)$ to the noise floor $\eta$.
